In order to define integrals to compute the flow of a vector field through a surface, we first need to define mathematically what it means to choose an “inside” and an “outside” for a surface. This brings us to orientation.

Orientation

Mathematically, a choice of orientation for a surface means a consistent choice of normal vector across the entire surface. We can think of the normal vector as giving the direction of positive flow through the surface.

Let be a surface in . An orientation on is a continuous function such that is a unit vector and is perpendicular at the point .

We say that the surface is orientable if such a function exists. We say that the surface is oriented if such a function has been chosen.

Based on the examples above, you might conjecture that every surface has exactly two orientations. This is almost true: any orientable, connected surface has exactly two orientations. However, there are some surfaces which are not orientable, as we will now see.

Non-orientable surfaces

For some surfaces , it’s impossible to define a continuous function such that is a unit vector and is perpendicular at the point . We say that these surfaces are non-orientable.

Our first example of a non-orientable surface is a möbius strip.

A möbius strip is a looped surface with a single twist, as pictured below.

Let’s see what happens when we try to chose an orientation on the möbius strip. If we choose a normal vector at a point, in order for to be continuous, this forces the choice of normal vector at nearby points.

Continuing this around the surface, we have a problem: we eventually get two normal vectors at the same point, pointing in opposite directions.

PICTURE

This is because it is impossible to choose unit normal vectors continuously on the möbius strip. Thus, the möbius strip is non-orientable.

You might also hear this described as “the möbius strip only has one side.”

Another, trickier example of a non-orientable surface is the Klein bottle. The Klein bottle actually exists in four dimensions, and can’t be embedded in three dimensions without intersecting itself. The Klein bottle has the same issue as the möbius strip. If we try to continuously choose a normal vector across the entire surface, we contradict ourselves, because the Klein bottle only has one side.

Fortunately, we will focus on orientable surfaces.
